# Web4 / Hestia vs. Bluesky

Bluesky is an important step toward a better social web. It gives people more control over identity, data hosting, moderation, and algorithmic reach. In simple terms, Bluesky is about portable speech.

Web4 and Hestia address a different layer: portable agency.

Where Bluesky asks, “Where does your social content live, and who controls what people see?”, Web4 asks, “Who is acting, under whose authority, according to what rules, with what proof, and where is the witnessed record?”

Hestia is the personal identity and authority layer. It gives a human, AI agent, device, or service a cryptographic identity, encrypted vault, delegation controls, multifactor device constellation, and witnessed trust record. It is the app you use to prove who you are, manage what your agents can do, and connect to communities or organizations.

A Web4 Hub is the connection point for a community, company, team, or society. A hub defines its own law, admits members, assigns roles, verifies credentials, handles encrypted member-to-hub communication, and records consequential actions in an append-only witnessed ledger. Hubs can also connect to other hubs, allowing societies to interoperate without surrendering identity or governance to a central platform.

The difference is not “another social network.” Web4 is infrastructure for trusted participation.

Bluesky gives users portable accounts and posts.

Web4 gives participants portable identity, authority, delegation, credentials, trust, and accountable action.

That matters because the next phase of the internet is not just humans publishing content. It is humans, AI agents, organizations, services, and devices acting on each other’s behalf. In that world, the hard problem is no longer only speech and reach. It is authority, consent, proof, and consequence.

Web4 is designed for that world.
